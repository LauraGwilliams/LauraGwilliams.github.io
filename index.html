A Short Introduction

I am currently a first year graduate student in the Cognition and Perception programme at NYU, co-supervised by Alec Marantz and David Poeppel.

Broadly speaking, my research attempts to bring together knowledge from theoretical linguistics, psychology and cognitive neuroscience in order to gain a comprehensive understanding of how the brain processes language and all its peculiarities. In specific terms, this falls into two camps of investigation. First is the connection between speech comprehension and access to stored representations at variant levels of complexity (e.g., the phoneme, morpheme and lexical item). The second camp investigates the processing of sub-lexical structure, across both spoken and written words. One of the most relevant questions being what units of a word are stored in memory versus what is computed in real-time.

For both of these questions I utilise a combination of electrophysiological techniques such as MEG and EEG, which provide unrivaled temporal precision. I conduct temporal-spatial and temporal-spectral analyses on these data in order to test the accuracy of different theoretically-charged processing models in accounting for observed neural responses.
