A Short Introduction

I am currently a first year PhD student in the Cognition and Perception programme at NYU, co-supervised by Alec Marantz and David Poeppel.

My research explores how the human brain encodes linguistic information, and how this information is accessed during language comprehension. So far I have primarily investigated the role of morphological structure for the processing of spoken and written words: Do morphemes stand in as "dictionary entries" that organise our lexicon? Do we form predictions based on probability distributions over root morphemes or whole words? How does the system recognise morphemes from letter sequences or speech streams?

More recently, my research has turned to the question of phonological ambiguity and its resolution. I am interested in investigating how a continuous auditory signal is so quickly categorised into discrete categories, and what additional information aids this process.

To address these questions I have thus far focused on utilising MEG in order to characterise the temporal progression of responses, and localise such responses with reasonable accuracy.
